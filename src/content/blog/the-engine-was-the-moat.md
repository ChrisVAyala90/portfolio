It took months of building to see what my company actually was. A few weeks later, twelve strangers reached the same conclusion on their own.

I spent those months on OrkaIntel, a graph-based AI system that helps fraud investigators surface the networks hiding across public data. The demand was documented. The state auditor's reports named the process gaps. The new Director of Program Integrity published a 56-page roadmap with one clear through-line, that the technology was outdated. Individuals working with overseers and investigators told me the same thing in plainer words. A senior state official engaged directly and liked what she saw.

The problem was real. The buyer was the obstacle. Selling into a government/state agency as a solo founder meant on-premises economics and procurement timelines measured in one to two years, at a point in my life with a young family and a second child on the way. I did that math honestly and stepped back from the state path.

The company had earned real looks along the way. Investigator groups had invited me to present it, and I had put it in front of startup competitions and accelerators to pressure-test the business itself. The feedback from one of those landed a few weeks after I made my call. A dozen experienced investors and operators, reviewing it independently, agreed the government buyer would be difficult to reach. Several went further, unprompted, and named the same asset I had already chosen to keep. The query layer on top, they wrote, is increasingly commoditized. The pattern library and the data fusion underneath are hard to build, and they carry across domains. The vertical was always something I could change. The engine was the moat.

The convergence of minds is the part I keep thinking about. When a dozen independent reviewers land on the read you reached alone at your desk, that is a signal worth trusting. It tells me the gut I built over my career is worth listening to.

That is the founder lesson I am carrying forward, into my day-to-day work and into the next ideas I evaluate. Demand and buying are two different things. Users can be clamoring for a solution while procurement, on-premises economics, and budget cycles keep the buyer from ever signing. I learned to respect that gap and to price it into any path forward. The engine I built stays in my back pocket while I look for its next application. And my family sits inside that calculus every time, which is exactly where they belong.
